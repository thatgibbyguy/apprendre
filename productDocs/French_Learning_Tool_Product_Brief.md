# Product Brief: French Learning Tool

**Document type:** Product Brief — Feature Set & Learning Methodology
**Target levels:** A1 through B2 (C1 available as stretch content)
**Date:** March 20, 2026

---

## 1. Product Vision

A French learning tool built around a simple observation: you can study French seriously for over a year and still not be able to say "uh oh, she's hiding" to your kid.

The problem isn't a lack of grammar knowledge. Existing tools teach grammar, vocabulary lists, and textbook dialogues — but almost none of them teach the actual language people use in everyday life. The mundane, reflexive phrases that make up 90% of real conversation — reacting to something surprising, making small talk, being playful with a child, narrating what's happening in front of you — are so ordinary that nobody thinks to teach them. The result is learners who can conjugate but can't converse.

This tool leads with functional spoken French. The kind of language parents use with kids, friends use over coffee, people produce without thinking. Grammar supports that goal — it doesn't lead. Structured grammar practice matters (especially for written French), but it serves the larger purpose of being able to actually talk to people.

The pedagogical engine draws from Second Language Acquisition research: comprehensible input, high-frequency chunks learned in context, meaningful tasks that push the learner to produce real language, and spaced retrieval for long-term retention. The tool adapts to the learner's current CEFR level across all four skills (listening, reading, speaking, writing), acknowledges that learners often have uneven profiles, and shifts the language of instruction from English-heavy at A1 to French-only by B2.

Target proficiency is B2 (conversational fluency). C1 content is included as lagniappe.

### 1.1 Design Principle: Spoken French as It's Actually Spoken

French as taught in textbooks and French as spoken by actual people diverge significantly. This tool teaches spoken French — which means:

- **"On" over "nous"** — Native speakers overwhelmingly use "on" in spoken French. This isn't regional dialect or casual slang; it's how the language works in practice. A child learning French in immersion school learns "on." The tool teaches "on" as the default for spoken contexts and "nous" as the formal/written form, not the other way around.
- **Reactive language** — Phrases like "oh là là," "ah bon?," "c'est pas vrai," "attends," "regarde," "qu'est-ce qui s'est passé?" are the connective tissue of real conversation. They are taught as high-priority chunks from A1 onward.
- **Everyday narration** — Describing what's happening right now ("elle se cache," "il est tombé," "attention, c'est chaud") is a core skill, not an afterthought. This is how parents talk to children, how friends narrate shared experiences, how people actually use the language.
- **Register reality** — Spoken French drops "ne" in negation ("je sais pas"), uses "y'a" for "il y a," contracts aggressively, and restructures sentences for emphasis ("c'est lui qui l'a fait"). The tool teaches the spoken register as primary and the written/formal register as a parallel skill, not the standard from which speech "deviates."
- **Simplified verb morphology as entry point** — Spoken French uses a far smaller set of verb forms than the full written paradigm. The passé simple, imperfect subjunctive, and many compound literary tenses exist in books but not in conversation. Louisiana French, West African French, and everyday Metropolitan French all converge on simpler, more regularized conjugation — and this is why those varieties are easier to understand. The tool teaches the verb forms people actually use in speech first. But it does not avoid "nous," irregular conjugations, or the full paradigm — if you want to actually test at B1, read a book, or write a proper email, you need all of it. The spoken forms are the *entry point*, not a ceiling. The full written paradigm is taught as a parallel literacy skill, introduced progressively alongside spoken practice.

This doesn't mean the tool ignores grammar or formal French. It means grammar is taught in service of real communication, and written French is treated as its own skill rather than the baseline that spoken French fails to meet. Vocabulary build-up is equally critical — chunks and high-frequency phrases get you talking, but a growing vocabulary is what lets you talk about more than the basics.

## 2. Core Learning Methodology

The pedagogical engine rests on four interlocking pillars. These aren't features a learner selects — they're principles embedded in every interaction.

### 2.1 Comprehensible Input at i+1

All content presented to the learner is calibrated to be 90–98% comprehensible, with the remaining percentage just beyond their current level, supported by context. The goal is input that is not just understandable but *compelling* — interesting enough that the learner's attention stays on meaning rather than on the effort of decoding.

Input alone is not enough. It serves as the foundation, not the entire structure.

### 2.2 Lexicogrammar: Patterns First, Rules Later

Vocabulary and grammar are taught together as high-frequency chunks — not isolated words, not abstract rules. Learners encounter patterns like "il y a," "je voudrais," "avoir besoin de" as whole units before any formal explanation.

The learning sequence follows the EPI (Extensive Processing Instruction) model:

1. **Modeling** — The learner encounters the target chunk in multiple meaningful contexts (input flood)
2. **Awareness-raising** — Attention is drawn to the pattern through noticing tasks (underlining, gap-fills, comparisons)
3. **Receptive processing** — High-intensity exposure through varied activities: narrow reading, translation, sentence completion
4. **Structured production** — The learner produces the chunk in controlled contexts (sentence builders, substitution tasks)

Grammar rules appear only *after* the learner has already internalized the pattern through exposure — concise, contextual, and never as the starting point.

### 2.3 Pushed Output Through Meaningful Tasks

The learner must produce language that stretches them. This is where they discover gaps in their own knowledge. Tasks are designed to require precision, not just communication.

Critically, task output requirements are matched to CEFR text-type capacity:

| Level | Expected Output Type | Example |
|-------|---------------------|---------|
| A1 | Lists, isolated phrases, simple sentences | "Je m'appelle…," "J'habite à…" |
| A2 | Short connected sentences, simple descriptions | Describing daily routine, a short message |
| B1 | Connected paragraphs, narration, opinion-giving | Recounting an experience, explaining a preference |
| B2 | Detailed argumentation, abstract topics, register awareness | Debating a position, writing a structured essay |

Assigning a task that exceeds the learner's text-type capacity leads to frustration and communication breakdown. The tool enforces this alignment.

### 2.4 Spaced Retrieval Practice

The strongest evidence-backed technique for long-term retention. Every vocabulary item, chunk, and grammar structure is cycled through spaced review:

- Reviews are spaced over increasing intervals
- The learner must actively recall (not re-read or recognize)
- Older material is revisited alongside newer material
- Retrieval context varies: different sentence frames, different task types, different directions (French → English, English → French)

## 3. Feature Set: Learning Modes

The tool offers four distinct modes. Conversation practice is the primary mode — it's what the tool is fundamentally *for*. The other three modes exist to build the skills that make conversation possible. The learner chooses the activity, not the pedagogy.

### 3.1 Conversation Practice (Primary Mode)

This is the core of the tool. Natural spoken dialogue in French, adapted to the learner's level, grounded in the situations where people actually use language.

**Scenario-based, not topic-based.** Conversations are built around functional scenarios drawn from real life, not abstract topics. Examples by level:

| Level | Example Scenarios |
|-------|------------------|
| A1 | Playing with a child ("regarde!" "oh non, il est tombé!"), greeting a neighbor, ordering coffee, reacting to something ("ah bon?" "c'est vrai?") |
| A2 | Describing what happened today, making plans with a friend, small talk at school pickup, narrating what's happening ("elle se cache," "attends, je cherche") |
| B1 | Explaining a problem to a repairperson, telling a story about something funny that happened, giving your opinion at dinner, comforting someone |
| B2 | Debating a topic, navigating a misunderstanding, handling an administrative phone call, expressing nuanced disagreement |

**Rapid, in-the-moment feedback.** This is a critical gap in existing tools. Kwiziq batches feedback into sets of 10 questions, which means you might get several unrelated topics wrong at once and have to stop and study multiple things simultaneously. This tool provides feedback *within the conversation* — not after a batch. Corrective feedback uses three calibrated strategies:

- **Recasts** (rephrasing the error correctly within the flow) — used when maintaining conversational momentum matters
- **Prompts** ("Can you try that verb form again?") — used when the learner is developmentally ready to self-correct; research shows these lead to more self-repair than recasts
- **Metalinguistic cues** ("Watch the gender agreement here") — used when explicit knowledge helps, especially at B1+

After each conversation, a brief summary identifies error patterns and areas to work on.

**Language of instruction shifts by level:**

| Level | Instruction Language |
|-------|---------------------|
| A1 | Primarily English, with French phrases introduced gradually |
| A2 | Mixed — French for familiar interactions, English for new concepts |
| B1 | Predominantly French; English only for complex grammar explanations |
| B2 | French only |

### 3.2 Structured Lessons (Grammar in Service of Conversation)

Teaches a specific grammar point, vocabulary set, or skill using the input flood → noticing → pushed output sequence. Grammar is never taught in isolation — always contextualized through example sentences, short texts, or dialogues.

**Lesson flow:**

1. **Pre-task** — Orient the learner to the topic and activate background knowledge
2. **Input flood** — Multiple encounters with the target structure in meaningful context
3. **Noticing** — Guided attention to form (highlighting, gap-fills, transformation tasks)
4. **Pushed output** — The learner produces language using the target structure, with planning time provided
5. **Feedback** — Targeted correction on the specific structure being practiced

### 3.3 Vocabulary & Chunk Drilling

Spaced retrieval practice for vocabulary and formulaic sequences. Design principles:

- **Always in context** — No isolated word lists. Every item appears in a sentence.
- **Always with the article** — Every noun is learned as "le chat," never just "chat." This is non-negotiable for French.
- **Varied task types** — Translation, gap-fill, sentence completion, definition in French (at higher levels)
- **Both directions** — French → English and English → French
- **Chunks prioritized** — High-frequency phrases taught as whole units: "il y a," "en fait," "à mon avis," "avoir besoin de," and at higher levels: "il s'agit de," "en revanche"

### 3.4 Interactive Exercises

Structured, repeatable practice tools for specific skills:

- **Conjugation trainers** — Timed and untimed, targeting the verb forms relevant to the learner's level
- **Gender practice** — Matching, sorting, and recall exercises for noun gender
- **Sentence builders** — Substitution tables and construction tasks for routinizing patterns
- **Timed retrieval challenges** — Speed-building exercises for automaticity
- **Task repetition with variation** — Similar tasks with different content or constraints to build flexible use

## 4. Adaptive Level System

### 4.1 Initial Assessment

The tool assesses level through a brief conversational interaction, not a formal test. The learner is asked to:

1. Introduce themselves in French
2. Describe what they did yesterday
3. Express an opinion about something simple

Based on vocabulary range, grammatical control, and fluency, the tool estimates their CEFR level. The assessment is shared honestly — no inflation — and the learner can override it.

### 4.2 Uneven Skill Profiles

Learners often read at one level and speak at another. The tool acknowledges this explicitly and targets the weaker skills while maintaining engagement through the stronger ones.

### 4.3 Periodic Reassessment

Level is reassessed periodically as the learner progresses, based on performance across modes rather than a single test.

## 5. Feedback & Correction Framework

### 5.1 Error Prioritization by Level

Not every error is corrected. The tool focuses on errors that are developmentally appropriate and communicatively significant:

| Level | Priority Errors |
|-------|----------------|
| A1–A2 | Gender (le/la), basic verb forms (être/avoir), être vs. avoir in passé composé, pronunciation of silent consonants |
| A2–B1 | Passé composé vs. imparfait (the #1 anglophone struggle), object pronoun placement, agreement in compound tenses |
| B1–B2 | Subjunctive triggers and formation, conditional structures, relative pronouns (dont, auquel), register awareness |
| B2 | Nuanced connector usage, avoiding anglicisms, idiomatic precision, formal vs. informal register switching |

### 5.2 Writing Feedback: Staged Process

Writing practice follows a draft → targeted feedback → revision cycle:

1. The learner produces a draft
2. Feedback targets a small number of specific error types (not everything at once)
3. The learner revises
4. Error focus expands gradually over time

### 5.3 Common Anglophone Error Tracking

The tool specifically watches for and addresses recurring anglophone errors:

- "Je suis [age]" instead of "J'ai [x] ans"
- Misuse of "c'est" vs. "il est"
- Adjective word order (most follow the noun; BANGS exceptions)
- Overuse of "parce que" — progressive introduction of "car," "puisque," "étant donné que"
- Direct translation of English phrasal verbs

## 6. Content Architecture

### 6.1 Organization by Functional Situation

Content is organized by what the learner needs to *do in real life* — not by grammar category and not by abstract communicative functions. The organizing principle is: what situations does a person actually find themselves in, and what do they need to be able to say?

**Priority content domains** (these drive chunk selection, grammar sequencing, and conversation scenarios):

- **Parenting & family life** — Playing with a child, narrating what's happening, reacting, comforting, giving instructions, being silly. This is high-frequency spoken French that no textbook teaches.
- **Daily social exchanges** — Small talk with neighbors, school pickup chat, making/canceling plans, catching up with friends, reacting to news.
- **Errands & transactions** — Ordering food, dealing with a shopkeeper, calling about an appointment, handling a minor problem.
- **Narrating & reacting** — Telling someone what happened, responding to the unexpected, expressing surprise/frustration/delight in real time.
- **Opinions & discussion** — Giving your take on something, agreeing/disagreeing, explaining why, navigating a light debate.
- **Written French** — Email, text messages, internet comments, and formal writing. Treated as its own register, not as the baseline.

Lexicogrammar chunks and grammar structures are introduced in service of these situations. For example, the passé composé isn't introduced because "it's the next grammar unit" — it's introduced because the learner needs to tell someone what happened yesterday.

### 6.2 French-Specific Content by Level

| Level | Key Grammar | Key Vocabulary Domains | French-Specific Challenges |
|-------|------------|----------------------|---------------------------|
| A1 | Present tense (être, avoir, -er verbs), formulaic phrases, imperative (regarde, attends, viens) | Reactive language, family, daily objects, food, basic narration ("il est tombé," "elle se cache") | Gendered nouns, French "r," nasal vowels, tu/vous |
| A2 | Passé composé, futur proche, common irregulars, basic object pronouns, negation (spoken: "je sais pas") | Home, work, hobbies, weather, health, small talk, making plans | PC vs. imparfait awareness, past participle agreement with être, identical-sounding verb endings |
| B1 | Imparfait contrast, conditional, relative pronouns, subjunctive intro, discourse connectors | Storytelling, opinion-giving, problem-describing, comforting, explaining | PC/imparfait mastery, object pronoun order, subjunctive triggers |
| B2 | Full subjunctive, plus-que-parfait, futur antérieur, passive, advanced pronouns (y, en, dont), all conditional types | Abstract and topical vocabulary, register-specific lexicon, nuanced disagreement, humor | Register switching, idiomatic expressions, false friends, literary tense recognition (passé simple — recognize, don't produce) |

### 6.3 Recycling

Target structures and chunks are recycled extensively across sessions and modes. Acquisition requires repeated meaningful encounters, not just conscious rule learning.

## 7. Assessment Strategy

### 7.1 Formative (Ongoing)

Continuous, low-stakes assessment embedded in every session. Diagnoses gaps, guides the next activity, and tracks progress against intermediate CEFR descriptors. Methods include performance in drills, error patterns in conversation, and retrieval success rates.

### 7.2 Summative (Benchmarks)

Optional periodic benchmarks aligned with DELF standards (A1–B2). These evaluate across all four skills: listening, reading, speaking, and writing. The learner receives an honest assessment of where they stand.

### 7.3 Session Structure

Each session follows a consistent arc:

1. **Opening** — Greeting in French (level-appropriate), recall of previous session, goal-setting
2. **Core work** — In the chosen mode, with embedded formative assessment
3. **Closing** — Summary of what was covered, 2–3 specific strengths, 1–2 areas to focus on next, a concrete self-study suggestion

## 8. Product Decisions

### 8.1 Platform

Web and mobile. Both platforms share the same learning engine and content; the interface adapts to the device. Progress syncs across platforms.

### 8.2 Audio & Pronunciation

Audio input and output are in scope and considered essential. The tool should provide real-time spoken feedback — pronunciation evaluation, spoken conversation practice, and audio playback of native speech. This is a core differentiator, not an optional add-on. Text-based phonetic guidance (IPA notation, rules of thumb like "CaReFuL" for final consonants) supplements the audio but does not replace it.

*Engineering decision needed:* Speech recognition and TTS engine selection, latency requirements for real-time conversation, and pronunciation scoring methodology.

### 8.3 Authentic Materials & Content Sourcing

The learner has access to a substantial body of Louisiana French materials. Additional authentic content will be sourced from publicly available French-language media: YouTube channels, Reddit communities (r/French, r/Cajun, francophone subreddits), French-language newspapers, and podcasts. At B1+, authentic materials become a primary input source alongside structured content.

Louisiana French deserves particular attention — it is a living variety with distinct vocabulary, pronunciation, and cultural context that standard Metropolitan French curricula ignore. The tool should acknowledge and incorporate this variety rather than treating it as deviation.

*Decision needed:* Content curation workflow, any licensing considerations for specific sources, and how Louisiana French content is tagged and integrated alongside standard French.

### 8.4 MVP Scope

The MVP combines two approaches that currently exist only in separate products:

**From Kwiziq's model** — Structured, CEFR-aligned grammar and vocabulary practice with AI-driven diagnostics. Kwiziq's strength is its "Brainmap" concept: tracking what the learner knows and doesn't know across granular grammar topics, then serving targeted practice. The tool adopts this diagnostic precision but wraps it in the EPI methodology (patterns first, rules later) rather than Kwiziq's grammar-first sequencing.

**From TalkPal's model** — Conversational AI practice with real-time voice interaction, roleplays, and varied speaking modes (chat, call, debate, character interactions). TalkPal's strength is making spoken practice feel low-stakes and genuinely engaging. The tool adopts this conversational engine but grounds it in the research methodology — corrective feedback is calibrated by type (recasts, prompts, metalinguistic cues), not generic, and conversation topics are matched to the learner's CEFR text-type capacity.

**What's different here:** Neither product does both well. Kwiziq is rigorous on grammar but has no meaningful conversation practice. TalkPal is engaging for speaking but lacks structured grammatical progression — it doesn't systematically build accuracy. This tool integrates both under a single pedagogical framework drawn from the research: EPI builds the linguistic foundation through high-frequency chunks and structured grammar, and TBLT/conversation practice provides the real-world context where that foundation gets used. The research methodology is the connective tissue, not an afterthought.

The MVP ships all four learning modes (conversation, structured lessons, vocabulary drilling, interactive exercises) because the methodology requires them to work together. Content spans A1 through B2 from launch, with C1 as lagniappe — the learner may have uneven skills across levels and needs access to the full range from day one.

### 8.5 Progress Persistence

*Engineering decision.* Learner state — including CEFR level per skill, spaced repetition schedules, error history, and session logs — must persist across sessions and sync across platforms. The spaced retrieval system in particular requires reliable state management; without it, the review intervals break down and the strongest evidence-backed technique in the methodology fails.

### 8.6 Monetization

Free for now. This is a personal learning tool, not a commercial product at this stage. Monetization decisions can be revisited if the tool is opened to other learners.

### 8.7 Competitive Positioning

The tool is differentiated by its integrated methodology:

**vs. Duolingo** — Duolingo gamifies learning but teaches shallow, decontextualized content. Streaks and XP replace actual pedagogical design. This tool prioritizes depth over engagement metrics.

**vs. Kwiziq** — Kwiziq offers strong CEFR-aligned grammar diagnostics and a detailed skill map, but it has two fundamental problems. First, it's grammar-first and not conversational — it teaches the rules of French but not the language people actually speak. Second, its feedback loop is too slow: questions come in batches of 10, so you might get several unrelated topics wrong at once and have to stop and study multiple things simultaneously. This tool uses Kwiziq-style diagnostic precision but wraps it in evidence-based methodology with rapid, in-the-moment feedback — patterns before rules, chunks before grammar labels, and corrections within the conversation rather than after a batch.

**vs. TalkPal** — TalkPal excels at conversational AI practice with voice interaction, roleplays, and varied speaking modes. But it lacks structured grammatical progression and doesn't systematically build accuracy. Its feedback is generic rather than calibrated to the learner's developmental readiness. This tool uses TalkPal-style conversational engagement but grounds every interaction in the research framework.

**vs. Babbel** — Babbel is structured but rigid, with a fixed lesson path that doesn't adapt to individual skill gaps or uneven profiles.

**vs. iTalki** — iTalki provides human tutors, which is ideal but expensive and scheduling-dependent. This tool provides the same calibrated feedback methodology (recasts, prompts, metalinguistic cues) that good human tutors use, available on demand.

**The core differentiator:** No existing product combines TalkPal's conversational audio practice with Kwiziq's diagnostic grammar rigor under a unified, research-backed pedagogical framework. This tool does.

## 9. Resolved: Regional Variation, Content Pipeline, Audio, and Timeline

### 9.1 Louisiana French Integration

Louisiana French is blended into the main learning path, not a separate track. It appears as regional variation alongside other francophone accents — the way a grammar exercise might show how something is said in Toulouse, Paris, and New Orleans. This approach treats French as a living, geographically diverse language rather than a monolithic Parisian standard.

Critically, many features associated with "Louisiana French" are simply features of *spoken French everywhere*. The tool doesn't frame these as regional quirks — it teaches them as standard spoken practice:

- **"On" instead of "nous"** — This is universal in spoken French. A child in a French immersion school in Louisiana learns "on." So does a child in Lyon. The tool teaches "on" as the spoken default and "nous" as the formal/written form.
- **Dropped "ne" in negation** — "Je sais pas" is how people talk. "Je ne sais pas" is how people write. Both are taught; spoken register comes first.
- **Contracted forms** — "Y'a" for "il y a," "t'as" for "tu as," "j'suis" for "je suis." These aren't sloppy French; they're spoken French.

**Simpler verb conjugation as starting point.** Louisiana French is far easier than Metropolitan textbook French, and the reason matters: it uses simpler, more regularized verb morphology. Fewer irregular forms in practice, less reliance on complex literary tenses, more analytic constructions. This isn't a regional accident — it's a pattern shared by West African French, Québécois French, and conversational Metropolitan French alike. The full conjugation paradigm taught in textbooks (nous parlâmes, vous eussiez parlé) is a written artifact that native speakers don't use in conversation.

This is why West African French is the easiest variety to understand — it tends toward the same simplification. And it's why the tool's spoken-first approach isn't just a preference but a pedagogical decision: teaching the verb forms people actually use in conversation gets learners speaking faster.

However, the tool does not avoid "nous," irregular verbs, or the full written paradigm. To actually achieve B1 on a DELF exam, you need command of irregular conjugations, the nous/vous forms, and the formal structures. The simpler spoken forms are the *entry point* — they get you producing language quickly — but the complete grammar is taught progressively for reading, writing, and testing. Vocabulary build-up runs in parallel: chunks get you talking, but a growing and deepening vocabulary is what lets you talk about anything beyond the basics.

The local colloquialisms of Louisiana French (specific vocabulary, particular expressions, greetings) are not the focus. The simpler verb conjugation is — because that simpler conjugation works everywhere in spoken French.

Where Louisiana French genuinely diverges from Metropolitan spoken French, those are presented as regional enrichment — optional, visible, and never treated as incorrect. The tool is not going to teach "comment les haricots?" as a greeting, but it will surface Louisiana-specific usage where it adds value.

Louisiana French content is optional but visible — a learner focused purely on Metropolitan French can stay in that lane, but the regional examples are always available.

### 9.2 Content Curation Pipeline

Content ingestion is largely one-off. Authentic materials from YouTube, Reddit, newspapers, and other sources are curated in batches, not continuously updated. The content library grows in periodic passes rather than through a live pipeline. This is a personal tool, not a newsroom — the materials need to be good and well-tagged, not fresh daily.

### 9.3 Audio Engine & Accent Selection

The accent landscape for this tool prioritizes three regional profiles that are closest to Louisiana French phonology:

- **Toulouse / Southern France** — shares some vowel qualities and intonation patterns with Louisiana French
- **Eastern Canada (Québécois/Acadian)** — historically related to Louisiana French through Acadian migration; shares vocabulary and certain phonological features
- **West Africa (particularly Senegal, Côte d'Ivoire)** — clear enunciation, often highly comprehensible to Louisiana French speakers

The learner reports understanding West African French speakers best. The tool should expose the learner to all three regional accents in audio content and conversation practice, alongside standard Metropolitan French. This aligns with the CEFR Companion Volume's emphasis on plurilingual competence — learners benefit from hearing multiple varieties rather than being trained on a single accent.

*Engineering decision still needed:* Which TTS and speech recognition engines best handle these accent varieties? Can the conversation mode offer accent selection for the AI interlocutor?

### 9.4 Content Timeline

All levels (A1 through B2, with C1 as lagniappe) ship together. There is no phased rollout by level — the full content scope is the MVP. This makes sense for a personal tool where the learner may have uneven skills across levels and needs access to the full range from day one.
